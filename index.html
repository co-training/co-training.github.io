Sim-and-Real Co-Training: A Simple Recipe for Vision-Based Robotic Manipulation

This work presents a simple recipe for effectively utilizing simulation data for real-world vision-based manipulation tasks.

We collect a modest number of demonstrations in real-world environments and use existing simulation frameworks and data generation tools to generate large-scale synthetic data in simulation. Across all embodiments and tasks that we study, simulation can offer a substantial improvement in performance even without perfect alignment to the real world. Furthermore, co-training can aid transfer even when the simulation and real-world data have significant discrepancies.

We compare co-training with different simulation data on five tasks across two robots. Co-training with Prior consistently boosts performance over policies trained on real data only. On top of these results, adding better aligned DC usually further improves the performance.

We use 4000 simulation demos and vary the number of real demos from 40 to 400. Even with 400 real demonstrations, the co-trained policy consistently outperforms the real-only policy, demonstrating the effectiveness of our co-training strategy.

Our co-training strategy makes use of sim-data that is only roughly aligned with real-world

Creating a perfect digital twin copy of the real-world task is challenging, requiring extensive manual tuning, system identification, and sourcing identical 3D assets. Instead, we opt to create tasks in simulation that share the same task semantics, namely the object categories in the environment and the same behaviors. We call these task-aware digital cousins.

Surprisingly, our co-training strategy can also make use of prior-sim datasets, that have significantly more diversity and even less alignment and tuning

We also take advantage of large-scale simulation datasets, which require no additional efforts on designing new tasks in simulation and collecting new data. These prior task-agnostic simulation datasets may have numerous discrepancies with real-world data, but they present a very simple and convenient way to leverage simulation data.

We conduct a study to understand which dataset composition factors in the simulation and real-world datasets matter the most, resulting in our simple recipe

Based on our empirical findings, we provide a set of recommendations to help practitioners reap the benefits of co-training with synthetic simulation data.

• Task and scene composition. The best results are achieved when co-training with simulation data from task-aware digital cousins, where the task and scene compositions closely resemble those of real-world tasks. However, co-training with large multi-task prior simulation data—where task and scene compositions differ from real-world settings—can still be beneficial.

• Object composition and initialization distribution. Incorporating diverse objects and varying their placements in simulation data helps real-world policies generalize to unseen scenarios.

• Alignment between the task-aware digital cousin and the real world. It is essential for the simulation task to share the same definition and success criteria as its real-world counterpart. Additionally, ensuring similar camera viewpoints between simulation and real-world settings improves performance, though perfect alignment is not necessary.

• Co-training hyperparameters. Utilize a sufficiently large amount of simulation data (e.g., orders of magnitude more than real-world data). Additionally, careful tuning of the co-training ratio is critical for optimizing performance.

Co-Training Ratio

Effect of the different co-training ratios, i.e., the percentage of simulation data sampled in each training step. We experiment on CupPnP task with 20 real demos and 1000 simulation demos from task-aware digital cousins. Tuning the co-training ratio is important for good performance of co-trained policies.

Camera Alignment

Visualization of the default and aligned camera views. To evaluate the importance of this alignment, we rendered data using the default, unaligned camera view and trained policies on the resulting misaligned simulation data. The results indicate a significant drop in performance compared to policies co-trained with properly aligned DC data. On the Panda arm CounterToSinkPnP task, the cotraining success rate dropped from 67% to 56%, while in the GR-1 humanoid CupPnP task, it declined from 95% to 70%. Notably, the aligned camera is not strictly identical to the real-world camera.

Sim-and-Real Co-Training Pipeline

Sim-and-Real Co-training Pipeline. Left: We start with a real-world task in mind and some prior simulation data. Middle: Given real-world tasks and prior simulation data, we can build additional digital cousin tasks that share semantic similarities with their real-world counterparts but may still have discrepancies in visual and physical features. From here we can consolidate prior simulation data, digital cousin data, and real world data. Right: We co-train on a mixture of real-world and simulation data. We sample simulation data according to a sampling ratio of α, and as we show in experiments, this sample ratio is crucial. After training the policy, we deploy the resulting policy directly in the real world.
